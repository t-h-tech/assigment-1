Alula Hegra

Al-Hegra archaeological site in Saudi Arabia is recognized as the kingdom's first UNESCO World Heritage Site.Human life evidence at the site dates back to the first millennium BCE. Al-Hegra is known for over 110 tombs carved by the Nabataeans into the rock formations to bury individuals of high status within the Nabataean civilization. Inscriptions and writings are clearly visible on some of the tombs, narrating details about the historical figures buried there, including healers, distinguished military figures, local leaders, and many others.

AlUla Old Town

The Old Town of AlUla, established in the 12th century, served as a crucial stop along ancient caravan routes laden with frankincense and as a key resting station on the pilgrimage route from Damascus to Mecca. Its towering castle, built approximately in the 10th century, stands at 45 meters high, functioning as a watchtower and a fortification for the town's wall, which featured 14 gates. Together, these narratives paint a picture of AlUla's historical significance as a commercial and spiritual hub, steeped in centuries of tradition and heritage.

Khaybar

Nestled among volcanic rock formations, Khaybar stands as one of the Arabian Peninsula's oldest inhabited archaeological sites, boasting a unique geological landscape shaped over millennia. This oasis of life, blessed with fresh waters and fertile lands, nurtures diverse flora and fauna, and fruit-bearing trees. Khaybar's welcoming and hospitable environment has supported human settlements and the blossoming of civilizations since time immemorial, making it a natural wonder and a historical beacon in the desert.
